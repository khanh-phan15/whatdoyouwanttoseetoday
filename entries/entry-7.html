CA TRÙ - VIETNAMESE CHAMBER MUSIC

Ca trù (Vietnamese ceremonial singing) is a traditional art form that originated in northern Vietnam. It involves a female vocalist who sings poetic lyrics accompanied by a dan day (a long-necked lute) and trống (drums), with the performer often using wooden clappers for rhythm. Ca trù was traditionally performed in royal courts, temples, and private gatherings, focusing on themes of love, morality, and social issues. Known for its intricate vocal techniques and sophisticated rhythms, ca trù was recognized as a UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage.

    Watching ca trù is like stepping into a world of quiet elegance and profound emotion. The music feels almost haunting in its beauty, with the soft, melodic voice of the ca trù singer weaving through the air, accompanied by the delicate plucking of the đàn đáy and the rhythmic tapping of the trống chầu. The singer’s voice is powerful yet fragile, often shifting from spoken word to song, and each note carries a deep sense of history and longing. What makes ca trù so captivating is its intimacy. The performance often feels like a personal conversation between the singer and the listener, as if each song is a direct expression of the performer’s emotions or a glimpse into the soul of Vietnam’s past. The lyrics, often poetic and metaphorical, reflect themes of love, loss, and longing, making it both a musical and emotional journey. There’s a certain mystery to ca trù, too—its origins are deeply rooted in the history of Vietnam, and watching it evokes a sense of connection to a past that feels distant but still alive in the music. It’s a rare and deeply enriching experience that makes you appreciate not just the art form, but also the cultural resilience that has kept it alive for centuries.
